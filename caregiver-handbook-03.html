Our Mission & Values

The Practice's mission is to provide high-quality, evidence-based ABA therapy that empowers children to build meaningful skills, develop independence, and participate fully in their families, schools, and communities.

Core Values

  • Family-centered care: Families are partners in every decision. Your input shapes goals, strategies, and priorities.
  • Evidence-based practice: Every intervention we use is grounded in the scientific principles of Applied Behavior Analysis and supported by peer-reviewed research.
  • Individualized treatment: No two children are the same. Treatment plans are tailored to your child's unique strengths, needs, and family context.
  • Ethical practice: We adhere to the BACB Ethics Code for Behavior Analysts and all applicable state licensure requirements.
  • Transparency: We strive to keep you informed about what we are doing, why we are doing it, and how it is working.
  • Dignity and respect: We treat every child and family with kindness, cultural sensitivity, and unconditional positive regard.
  • Continuous improvement: We regularly evaluate our outcomes, seek feedback, and invest in ongoing training for our team.

Non-Discrimination & Cultural Responsiveness

The Practice is committed to providing services in an environment free from unlawful discrimination, harassment, and bias. We do not discriminate in the provision of services based on race, color, national origin, ancestry, age, religion, disability, sex, sexual orientation, gender identity or expression, marital status, genetic information, veteran status, immigration status, or any other characteristic protected by law.

We recognize that every family brings unique cultural values, traditions, and communication styles. Our team strives to:

  • Learn about and respect your family's cultural background, language preferences, and values
  • Incorporate culturally responsive practices into treatment planning
  • Provide materials and communication in your preferred language when possible
  • Avoid making assumptions based on diagnosis, background, or family structure

If you feel that you or your child has experienced discrimination or cultural insensitivity from any member of our team, please contact our administrative office immediately. We take all reports seriously.

What We Do: ABA Therapy

Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) is the only therapy for Autism Spectrum Disorder that is classified as an evidence-based best practice by the U.S. Surgeon General and the American Psychological Association. ABA uses scientific principles of learning and behavior to teach new skills, reduce barriers to learning, and improve quality of life.

What ABA is

  • Individualized, data-driven therapy tailored to your child's specific needs and goals
  • Focused on building functional skills: communication, social interaction, self-care, play, safety, and adaptive behavior
  • Delivered one-to-one by trained therapists under the supervision of a Board Certified Behavior Analyst (BCBA)
  • Based on positive reinforcement — teaching through motivation, not punishment
  • Measured continuously using objective data so we always know if treatment is working

What ABA is not

  • ABA is not academic tutoring. While skills learned in ABA often improve school performance, academic instruction is outside our scope.
  • ABA does not use aversive procedures. The Practice is committed to non-aversive, trauma-informed, assent-based care.
  • ABA does not attempt to change who your child is. The goal is to give your child the skills they need to express themselves, stay safe, and participate in the activities and relationships that matter to them and your family.

Scope of services

The Practice provides services exclusively within the scope of ABA. Goals are determined based on assessment results, caregiver input, medical necessity, and clinical judgment. We do not implement non-evidence-based interventions. If another provider recommends an approach that falls outside ABA, your clinical team will discuss it with you and help you make an informed decision.

Your Child's Care Team

Your child's care team includes clinical supervisors, mid-level clinicians, direct-care therapists, and administrative support staff. Because the Practice operates across multiple states, staff titles and credentials vary by jurisdiction. Below we describe each role by function so you understand who does what, regardless of the specific title used in your state.

Clinical Supervisor — Behavior Analyst (BCBA / LBA)

Your child's primary clinician is a Board Certified Behavior Analyst (BCBA) who is licensed in the state where services are provided. In New York and New Jersey, this license is called a Licensed Behavior Analyst (LBA). In Virginia, it is also called an LBA. All BCBAs hold a master's degree (minimum) in behavior analysis or a related field, have completed extensive supervised clinical experience, and are nationally certified by the Behavior Analyst Certification Board (BACB).

Your BCBA is responsible for:

  • Conducting the initial and ongoing behavioral assessments
  • Developing, reviewing, and updating the individualized treatment plan
  • Training, supervising, and evaluating all direct-care staff working with your child
  • Providing caregiver training and coaching
  • Collecting and analyzing data to measure progress and adjust strategies
  • Coordinating with other providers (school, physicians, therapists)
  • Leading team meetings, progress reviews, and reassessments
  • Making all clinical decisions about your child's treatment

Mid-Level Clinician — Assistant Behavior Analyst

In some cases, a mid-level clinician may assist the BCBA with treatment plan implementation, data analysis, and staff supervision. The specific credential depends on your state:

  • New York: Certified Behavior Analyst Assistant (CBAA) — a New York state credential for individuals who have completed specific coursework and supervision requirements and work under the direct oversight of an LBA.
  • New York: Limited permit holders — individuals who have applied for LBA licensure and are authorized to practice under supervision while their application is processed.
  • New Jersey: Board Certified Assistant Behavior Analyst (BCaBA) — nationally certified by the BACB, holds a bachelor's degree in behavior analysis, and practices under BCBA supervision.
  • Virginia: Licensed Assistant Behavior Analyst (LABA) — a Virginia state license for individuals who hold a BCaBA credential and meet state requirements.

All mid-level clinicians work under the supervision and clinical authority of a BCBA/LBA. They do not make independent clinical decisions.

Direct-Care Therapist — Behavior Technician / Behavior Interventionist

Your child's direct-care therapist is the team member who provides one-to-one therapy during each session. At the Practice, we may refer to this role as a Behavior Technician (BT), Registered Behavior Technician (RBT), or Behavior Interventionist (BI) depending on their credential status. Responsibilities include:

  • Delivering therapy sessions in the home, community, or via telehealth
  • Implementing skill acquisition and behavior reduction programs exactly as designed by the BCBA
  • Collecting data on your child's behavior and skill development during every session
  • Communicating with the BCBA and your family about progress, concerns, and changes
  • Following all safety protocols and behavior intervention plans

Registered Behavior Technicians (RBTs) hold a national credential through the BACB and have completed a 40-hour training program and competency assessment. Behavior Technicians (BTs) are staff who are trained in-house and supervised at the same standard but may not yet hold the RBT credential. All direct-care staff receive ongoing training and supervision regardless of credential level.

Other Qualified Professionals

In certain circumstances and as permitted by state law, other licensed professionals may participate in your child's care:

  • Psychologists may conduct diagnostic evaluations, behavioral assessments, or provide consultation as part of the clinical team.
  • Licensed Mental Health Professionals (LMHPs) — In Virginia, certain behavioral services may be provided by LMHPs (Licensed Professional Counselors, Licensed Clinical Social Workers, and similar credentials) under appropriate supervision and within their scope of practice.
  • Special education professionals, speech-language pathologists, and occupational therapists may collaborate with your child's ABA team but are not employed by the Practice unless otherwise noted.

Clinical Leadership

All clinical programs are overseen by a Clinical Director who is a BCBA/LBA with advanced experience. The Clinical Director reviews treatment plans, ensures fidelity of services, manages clinical quality, and is available to address concerns that cannot be resolved at the team level.

Case Coordinator

Every family at the Practice is assigned a Case Coordinator — your single point of contact for everything related to your child's services. Your Case Coordinator is responsible for:

  • Making sure you are satisfied with services and that communication is working well
  • Ensuring the BCBA and behavior technician are a good fit for your family
  • Managing scheduling, rescheduling, and cancellations
  • Handling quality concerns — if you have an issue with a therapist, a session, or anything else, contact your Case Coordinator first
  • Coordinating between you, the clinical team, and the administrative office
  • Following up on missing documents, authorization timelines, and outstanding tasks
  • Escalating clinical concerns to the BCBA or Clinical Director as needed

Your Case Coordinator is not a clinician — they are an operations professional who ensures everything runs smoothly so the clinical team can focus on your child. If something is not working, tell your Case Coordinator. That is exactly what they are there for.

Administrative & Support Team

Our administrative team supports intake processing, insurance verification, authorization management, billing, and general client services. For billing or insurance questions, contact our revenue cycle team through the main office.

You have the right to know who is working with your child. Your child's assigned BCBA and direct-care staff will be introduced to you before or at the start of services. You may ask about any team member's qualifications, credentials, and experience at any time. If you have concerns about a specific staff member, please contact your BCBA or the administrative office.

Requesting a Different Therapist

If you feel that the therapeutic fit between your child and their assigned therapist is not working, you may request a change. Please discuss your concerns with your BCBA or Case Coordinator. We will do our best to accommodate your request, though changes are subject to staffing availability and may take time to arrange. Requests based on unlawful discrimination (race, gender, religion, etc.) cannot be honored.

Staff Credentialing & Supervision

All direct-care staff work under the clinical supervision of a licensed BCBA/LBA, regardless of their individual credential level. Supervision includes direct observation, feedback, data review, and ongoing training. Staff credentials and licensing requirements vary by state.

In New York, some clinicians may hold a limited permit — meaning they have completed the education and experience requirements for LBA licensure and are authorized by the state to practice under the direct supervision of a fully licensed LBA while their application is being processed. If a limited permit holder is assigned to your child's case, you will be informed and you may discuss any questions or concerns with your BCBA or Case Coordinator.

The Assessment Process

All services begin with a comprehensive behavioral assessment conducted by a BCBA. The purpose of the assessment is to understand your child as a whole person — their strengths, challenges, communication style, interests, family context, and the specific behaviors that are interfering with daily life.

What the assessment involves

  • Caregiver interview: A detailed conversation about your child's history, current skills, daily routines, behavioral concerns, medical background, and your goals for therapy.
  • Record review: Review of diagnostic evaluations, school records (IEP/504), medical records, and reports from other providers.
  • Standardized assessments: Your BCBA may administer formal tools such as the VB-MAPP, ABLLS-R, AFLS, Vineland-3, or other validated measures to assess your child's skills across domains.
  • Direct observation: The BCBA will observe your child in their natural environment (home, community, or school when possible) to see how they communicate, play, interact, and respond to everyday situations.
  • Functional behavior assessment (FBA): If your child engages in challenging behaviors, the BCBA will assess the function (purpose) of those behaviors to design effective, individualized interventions.

The assessment may take place over multiple sessions (typically 2–4 visits over 1–2 weeks). Both direct observation and indirect assessment methods are used.

Your role in the assessment

You are the expert on your child. Your input is essential throughout the assessment. You may be asked to complete questionnaires, provide examples of behaviors, share video, or participate in structured activities with your child and the BCBA. Please be transparent about your child's preferences, dislikes, medical needs, and any safety concerns.

Please let us know if your child is ill, has had a medication change, has not slept well, or is otherwise not themselves on the day of an assessment session. We want to see your child at their best, and we can always reschedule.

Treatment Planning & Authorization

After the assessment, your BCBA will develop an individualized treatment plan. This plan includes:

  • A summary of assessment results and your child's current skill levels
  • Specific, measurable treatment goals and objectives
  • Recommended number of therapy hours per week (based on medical necessity)
  • Behavior intervention plan (BIP) if applicable
  • Caregiver training goals
  • Supervision schedule
  • Criteria for mastery, maintenance, and generalization
  • Transition and discharge planning criteria

Treatment plan review

Before services begin, a meeting will be scheduled with you to review the proposed treatment plan. You will have the opportunity to ask questions, suggest changes, and ensure the plan reflects your family's priorities. You may be asked to sign the treatment plan indicating your agreement.

Insurance authorization

Once the treatment plan is complete, it is submitted to your insurance company for authorization. This process can take anywhere from a few days to several weeks depending on the payer. Services cannot begin until authorization is received. Your administrative team will keep you updated on the status.

Ongoing reassessment

Treatment plans are living documents. Your BCBA will reassess your child's progress regularly (typically every 6 months, or as required by your insurance) and update goals, strategies, and service hours based on data. You will be included in every reassessment review.

Caregiver Participation (Required)

Why this matters: Research consistently shows that children make the most progress when caregivers are active, consistent participants in treatment. Caregiver involvement is not just encouraged — it is a required component of ABA services. Most insurance payers require documentation of caregiver participation as a condition of continued authorization.

What caregiver participation looks like

  • Be present and available: A designated caregiver (18 or older) must be physically present in the home during all in-home sessions. This is non-negotiable for safety and clinical reasons.
  • Attend caregiver training: Your BCBA will provide ongoing training on how to implement strategies at home. This may include modeling, role-playing, practicing techniques with your child, and reviewing data together.
  • Implement strategies between sessions: The skills your child learns in therapy need to be practiced and reinforced throughout the day. Your consistency is the single biggest predictor of your child's success.
  • Participate in team meetings: Regular meetings will be held to review progress, discuss data, adjust goals, and address concerns. Your presence and input are essential.
  • Provide feedback: Tell us what is working, what is not, and what has changed. Your observations at home, school, and in the community are data we cannot collect without you.
  • Communicate about your child: Notify us of medication changes, sleep disruptions, illness, family events, school changes, or anything that might affect your child's behavior or availability.

What happens if caregiver participation is insufficient

If we observe a pattern of insufficient caregiver involvement — including not being present during sessions, not attending training, not implementing strategies, or not participating in team meetings — we will:

  1. Have an honest, non-judgmental conversation about barriers and how we can help
  2. Document the concern and create a plan to address it
  3. If the pattern continues despite support, the Practice reserves the right to reduce services, adjust the treatment plan, or, in some cases, discharge from services

We understand that life is complicated. If you are struggling with scheduling, transportation, work conflicts, or other barriers, please tell us. We will work with you to find solutions.

Sessions & Scheduling

Your child's session schedule is based on the number of hours recommended in the treatment plan and authorized by your insurance. The Practice will work with you to build a schedule that fits your family's life while meeting clinical recommendations.

Session structure

A typical session includes a mix of structured teaching, natural environment teaching (NET), play-based activities, and caregiver coaching. Your child's BT/RBT will follow the treatment plan designed by the BCBA. Sessions end with a brief summary for the caregiver and a signature on session notes.

BCBA supervision sessions

Your BCBA will regularly join sessions to observe, coach staff, model techniques for you, and collect data. BCBA supervision is a required component of ABA and is typically scheduled at a regular cadence (e.g., every 1–2 weeks depending on clinical need and payer requirements).

Hours of operation

Therapy sessions are scheduled based on your child's needs and may occur mornings, afternoons, or early evenings, Monday through Sunday. Administrative staff are generally available Monday through Friday, 8:00 AM to 5:00 PM. The Practice does not offer 24-hour on-call coverage.

If your child's availability does not allow for the recommended number of treatment hours, services may be placed on hold until adequate availability can be arranged. Under-utilizing authorized hours can also affect future insurance authorizations.

Attendance & Cancellation Policy

Consistent attendance is critical for treatment effectiveness. Missed sessions mean missed learning opportunities and can significantly slow your child's progress.

Cancellation requirements

  • Provide a minimum of 24 hours notice when canceling or rescheduling a session.
  • Contact your assigned therapist directly to cancel. If you cannot reach them, contact the office.
  • If a therapist arrives and the client is not home, unavailable, or unable to receive services, this counts as a no-show.

Consequences of excessive cancellations

  • Cancellation fee: No-shows and same-day cancellations may result in a fee. Cancellation fees are the caregiver's responsibility and are not covered by insurance.
  • Service reduction: Three (3) consecutive cancellations or five (5) total cancellations within one authorization period may result in reduction or termination of services.
  • Authorization impact: Insurance companies track utilization. If authorized hours are consistently not used, the payer may reduce future authorizations.

Therapist cancellations

If the Practice needs to cancel a session due to therapist illness, weather, training, or other circumstances, we will notify you as soon as possible and attempt to reschedule. If a therapist has scheduled time off, we will try to notify you at least two weeks in advance and arrange a substitute therapist when possible.

Substitute therapists

A substitute therapist may occasionally cover a session due to your primary therapist's illness, vacation, or training. Substitute therapists are supervised and have access to your child's treatment plan. We will notify you in advance whenever possible.

Illness Policy

All individuals in the service setting — including the client, caregivers, siblings, and other household members — must be symptom-free for at least 24 hours before participating in or being present during ABA therapy.

Cancel the session if anyone in the home has:

  • Vomiting or diarrhea (within the last 24 hours)
  • Fever of 100.4°F (38°C) or higher
  • Severe sore throat, persistent cough, or headache
  • Any communicable or contagious disease (chicken pox, influenza, strep, COVID-19, RSV, lice, conjunctivitis, hand-foot-mouth, etc.)
  • Undiagnosed rash

If illness requires cancellation, contact us as soon as possible. A physician's clearance may be required before returning to therapy after a communicable illness.

Therapists are also required to follow this policy. If your therapist is ill, we will cancel or send a substitute.

Inclement Weather, Holidays & Closures

Inclement weather

Severe weather — including snow, ice, storms, and extreme heat — may make it unsafe for therapists to travel. In these situations, either party may cancel without penalty. Your Case Coordinator will notify you as soon as possible if a therapist cannot safely travel to your home.

When an in-person session is canceled due to weather or emergency, a telehealth replacement session may be offered when clinically appropriate and covered by your insurance. This helps maintain treatment consistency and protects your authorized hours.

We encourage you to use good judgment and not take undue risks. The decision to proceed with or cancel a session in borderline weather is ultimately yours.

Holidays and closures

The Practice may be closed on certain federal and religious holidays. Your Case Coordinator will notify you of scheduled closures in advance whenever possible. Therapy sessions that fall on closure days will be rescheduled when possible.

In-Home Services

During home-based sessions, your home serves as our staff's workplace. We are committed to keeping both your family and our team safe. The following policies apply to all in-home sessions:

Caregiver presence

  • A designated caregiver aged 18 or older must be present in the home during all sessions. This person must be listed on your intake form or Alternate Caregiver Consent.
  • If no approved caregiver is present when the therapist arrives, the session will be canceled and may be counted as a no-show.

Environment

  • Please provide a clean, safe area for therapy with minimal distractions. The therapist may work in any appropriate room and may request use of a quiet space.
  • In some cases, the therapist may work with the child behind a closed door (such as the child's bedroom) depending on the target skill and environmental factors. This is standard clinical practice.
  • Secure all pets in a separate area during sessions unless specific arrangements are made with the therapist.
  • Smoking, vaping, and e-cigarettes are prohibited in the service area while services are being provided.
  • All weapons and firearms must be secured in a locked location inaccessible to children and staff during all sessions.
  • Please ensure the home is free from safety hazards (unsecured medications, sharp objects, broken glass, etc.).

Toileting and personal care

For routine diaper changes or toileting accidents that are not part of a treatment plan, the caregiver present during the session is responsible. Staff will notify the caregiver when assistance is needed.

When toileting is a treatment plan objective, your child's BCBA will design a specific toileting protocol. Direct-care staff will implement this protocol as part of therapy, which may include taking the child to the bathroom, managing accidents, providing reinforcement for successes, and collecting data. The protocol will be reviewed with you before implementation and you will be trained on how to continue the program between sessions.

Medications and medical needs

  • Therapists may not administer any medication under any circumstances, including over-the-counter medications. If your child needs medication during a session, the caregiver must administer it.
  • Notify us in writing of all allergies, medical conditions, dietary restrictions, and medication changes upon enrollment or as they arise.

Other household members

  • The Practice is not responsible for other children in the household who are not receiving services.
  • Siblings may be involved in sessions at the therapist's discretion for training purposes (e.g., practicing social skills).

Materials and supplies

  • Caregivers may be asked to provide or purchase materials for therapy (e.g., specific toys, craft supplies, visual supports). Your BCBA will discuss any needed materials in advance.
  • The Practice provides clinical materials, data sheets, and assessment tools as needed.

WiFi access

Therapists may request WiFi access to perform essential duties including accessing electronic health records, entering session data, and obtaining digital signatures. By allowing therapy in your home, you indicate willingness to provide WiFi access for clinical purposes.

Community-Based Services

When clinically appropriate, therapy may include activities in natural community settings such as parks, playgrounds, grocery stores, libraries, restaurants, or public transit. Community-based practice helps your child generalize skills learned at home to real-world environments.

Community outings policy

  • Community outings require prior written consent (included in the intake consent forms).
  • The Practice staff are strictly prohibited from driving clients. Transportation to and from community locations is the caregiver's responsibility.
  • With your consent, your child may walk, use public transportation, or use rideshare services accompanied by a therapist.
  • Outings will be planned in advance and aligned with your child's treatment goals.
  • In community settings, there is a possibility that aspects of your child's health information may be incidentally revealed (e.g., a therapist working with your child in public).

School Coordination

With your consent, your BCBA may coordinate with your child's school team to ensure consistency across settings. This may include:

  • Observing your child in the school environment
  • Consulting with teachers, special education staff, speech-language pathologists, and occupational therapists
  • Attending IEP or 504 meetings as part of the clinical team
  • Sharing relevant clinical information with school personnel (with your written authorization)
Important: Schools are covered by FERPA (not HIPAA). Sharing information with your child's school requires a separate HIPAA authorization, which is included in your intake consent forms if you selected school coordination. Once information is shared with the school, it may no longer be protected by HIPAA.

Please note that ABA services provided during school hours may not be covered by all insurance plans. Your administrative team will verify coverage before scheduling any school-based services.

Telehealth Services

Some ABA services may be delivered via interactive audio or video conferencing (telehealth) when clinically appropriate. Telehealth is most commonly used for caregiver coaching, treatment plan reviews, and certain behavioral assessments.

  • A caregiver must be present and actively available during all telehealth sessions.
  • Telehealth risks include potential for disrupted or distorted transmissions and technology issues.
  • Telehealth sessions will be conducted using HIPAA-compliant platforms.
  • You may withdraw consent for telehealth at any time without affecting your child's right to in-person services.
  • Telehealth may not be covered by all insurance plans or in all states. Your team will verify coverage before scheduling.

Safety & Emergency Procedures

Before the first session

Your intake form includes a safety screen. If your child has known safety concerns (elopement, aggression, self-injury, pica, water safety, danger awareness), please disclose them fully so our team can prepare appropriate safety plans.

During sessions

  • Therapists are equipped to handle common safety situations involving elopement, self-injurious behavior, and aggression.
  • Your child's treatment plan will include specific safety procedures if applicable.
  • In a medical or psychiatric emergency, staff will call 911 and provide basic care until help arrives. Staff will notify the caregiver immediately.

First aid

If your child is injured during a session, staff may provide basic first aid if the caregiver is unable to attend to the situation immediately. Staff provide first aid as good Samaritans and are not acting as medical providers. For any injury beyond basic first aid, the caregiver will be asked to take over or emergency services will be called.

If bodily fluids are present during a session due to injury, illness, self-injurious behavior, or toileting, the caregiver will be asked to attend to the situation. Staff will assist with behavior management as appropriate.

Incident reporting

Any injury, safety concern, or notable event during a session will be documented and communicated to the caregiver and BCBA. Significant incidents will be reviewed by the Clinical Director. You will be notified verbally the same day, and a written summary will be available upon request.

Behavior Management

Challenging behaviors are a normal part of ABA therapy. As your child learns new skills and old patterns are addressed, you may see temporary increases in certain behaviors before they improve. This is expected and is a sign that treatment is working.

Our approach

  • The Practice uses non-aversive, function-based, trauma-informed behavior management strategies.
  • We do not use punishment, physical aversives, withholding of meals, or any procedure that causes pain or humiliation.
  • All behavior interventions are based on the assessed function of the behavior and are designed to teach replacement skills — giving your child a better way to get what they need.
  • Your BCBA will explain the rationale and expected effects of all behavior strategies before implementing them, including the possibility of temporary increases in behavior (extinction bursts).
  • Staff use de-escalation techniques and the least restrictive approach possible in all situations.

During behavioral episodes

During active behavior management, please follow the therapist's guidance. Well-intentioned caregiver intervention can inadvertently reinforce the behavior the therapist is working to reduce. If a caregiver training session is in progress, the therapist will guide you on when and how to respond.

When safety is at risk

If a behavioral episode creates an immediate safety risk that cannot be managed through de-escalation and standard procedures, your therapist may use brief protective measures to prevent injury to your child, themselves, or others in the environment. These measures are intended to be the least restrictive option available and used for the shortest time necessary. Your BCBA will discuss any such event with you and update the behavior plan if needed. If anyone is in immediate danger, call 911.

Supervision & Quality Assurance

All direct-care staff (RBTs and BTs) are supervised by a BCBA in accordance with BACB requirements and applicable state regulations. Supervision includes:

  • Regular direct observation of therapy sessions
  • Feedback and coaching for the therapist
  • Review of data and treatment fidelity
  • Modeling of techniques for both staff and caregivers

Supervision ratios meet or exceed all BACB and state requirements. If you have concerns about the quality of services or a specific staff member, please contact your BCBA or the administrative office.

Collaboration with Other Providers

With your consent, the Practice will collaborate with your child's other providers to ensure coordinated care. This may include:

  • Pediatricians and primary care physicians
  • Psychiatrists and prescribing providers
  • Neurologists and developmental pediatricians
  • Speech-language pathologists
  • Occupational therapists
  • Physical therapists
  • Psychologists and counselors
  • School teams and special education staff
  • Care coordinators and case managers

The Practice will not implement non-evidence-based interventions recommended by other providers. If a conflict arises, your BCBA will discuss the situation with you transparently. If your child's needs fall outside our scope of practice, we will help you find appropriate resources.

Professionalism & Boundaries

The Practice maintains strict professional boundaries between staff and families. This protects both your family and our team. Staff are not permitted to:

  • Engage in personal or social relationships with clients, caregivers, or their families
  • Give or accept gifts with a monetary value exceeding $10 (per BACB Ethics Code 1.11). Small tokens of appreciation under $10 are acceptable.
  • Attend personal events such as birthday parties, graduations, or family gatherings in a personal capacity. However, clinical sessions may be held at social events (e.g., a birthday party, playground, family gathering) when targeting social skills or community-based goals as part of the treatment plan.
  • Provide services outside their job description, including babysitting, childcare, respite, tutoring, or personal errands
  • Share personal phone numbers, social media accounts, or personal email addresses
  • Discuss personal matters unrelated to your child's treatment

These boundaries apply during active services and after discharge. We understand this can feel impersonal, but these limits exist to protect the therapeutic relationship and comply with BACB ethical standards.

Social Media & Non-Service Encounters

Staff are prohibited from connecting with clients, caregivers, or family members on any social media platform (Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, LinkedIn, etc.). This protects your privacy and maintains professional boundaries.

If you encounter an the Practice staff member in public, they will not initiate contact to protect your privacy. You are welcome to say hello, but the staff member will not disclose or discuss your child's services in any public setting.

Communication Expectations

How we communicate with you

  • Session updates: Your therapist or BCBA will keep you informed about how sessions are going. This may include a brief verbal check-in, a written note, or updates through your Case Coordinator.
  • BCBA updates: Your BCBA is available to discuss progress, answer clinical questions, and address concerns during or after supervision sessions.
  • Progress reports: Written progress reports will be shared at each authorization period (typically every 6 months).
  • Administrative matters: Scheduling, billing, and authorization updates will be handled by our administrative team.

How to reach us

  • First point of contact for most things: Your assigned Case Coordinator — scheduling, rescheduling, cancellations, complaints, general questions, and any concerns about how services are going.
  • Clinical questions: Your assigned BCBA — treatment goals, behavior strategies, your child's progress, clinical recommendations.
  • Billing and insurance: The administrative office or revenue cycle team.
  • Urgent concerns: Call the main office. For true emergencies, call 911.

Email and text communication may be used for scheduling and non-urgent matters with your consent. Email and text are not appropriate for emergencies and may not be read immediately. Complex or sensitive topics should be discussed by phone or in person.

Technology & Data Collection

Continuity, Transition & Discharge

Our goal is to prepare your child for success beyond ABA therapy. From the first assessment, we plan for transition and eventual discharge.

Discharge criteria (positive outcomes)

  • Mastery of all age-appropriate treatment plan objectives
  • Demonstration of skills across settings with generalization and maintenance
  • Diagnostic criteria for ASD are no longer met (per diagnosing provider)
  • Successful transition to a less intensive service model

Services may be placed on hold or terminated if:

  • Staffing: We are unable to staff your child's case within 90 days or one authorization period
  • Payment: Outstanding co-pays, deductibles, or fees remain unpaid
  • Insurance: Coverage lapses or authorization is denied
  • Attendance: Excessive cancellations or consistent under-utilization of authorized hours
  • Caregiver involvement: Insufficient participation despite documented support efforts
  • Safety: Threats, harassment, or physical/verbal abuse directed at staff
  • Clinical appropriateness: The client is no longer benefiting from ABA services
  • Ethical concerns: Requests that would require staff to violate the BACB Ethics Code

The Practice will attempt to provide reasonable advance notice before terminating services, except when immediate safety concerns require earlier action. Upon discharge, we will provide a summary and recommendations for continued support when appropriate.

You may withdraw your child from services at any time by notifying us in writing. Upon withdrawal, any outstanding financial obligations remain in effect.

Confidentiality & HIPAA

The Practice complies with the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) and all applicable state privacy laws. We are dedicated to protecting your child's Protected Health Information (PHI).

What is PHI?

PHI is any information that identifies your child and relates to their health condition, treatment, or payment for services. This includes names, dates of birth, addresses, phone numbers, diagnosis, treatment records, session notes, and insurance information.

How we use PHI

We use PHI only as permitted by law, including for:

  • Treatment: Sharing information among your child's care team
  • Payment: Submitting claims and obtaining authorization from your insurance
  • Operations: Quality assurance, training, compliance, and business functions
  • As required by law: Mandated reporting, court orders, public health requirements

We will not use or disclose PHI for any other purpose without your written authorization. You may revoke any authorization at any time in writing.

Our full Notice of Privacy Practices is provided as part of the intake consent process and is available upon request at any time.

Grievances & Complaints

If you have a concern about any aspect of your child's services, we want to hear about it. You may:

  1. Talk to your Case Coordinator — They are your first point of contact for any concern and will work to resolve it or escalate appropriately.
  2. Talk to your BCBA — For clinical concerns about treatment, goals, or staff performance.
  3. Contact our administrative office — For issues that are not resolved by the clinical team, or for concerns about billing, scheduling, or staff conduct.
  4. Submit a written complaint — We will acknowledge receipt and work to provide a response in a timely manner.
  5. File with the BACB — If you believe a behavior analyst has violated ethical standards, you may file a complaint with the Behavior Analyst Certification Board at bacb.com.
  6. File with the state licensing board — Each state has a licensing board that oversees behavior analysts.
  7. File a HIPAA complaint — If you believe your privacy rights have been violated, you may file a complaint with the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services Office for Civil Rights within 180 days.

the Practice will not retaliate against you for filing any complaint. Your services will not be affected by raising a concern in good faith.

Mandated Reporting

All the Practice staff are mandated reporters under applicable state law. This means that if any staff member has reasonable cause to suspect child abuse, neglect, or endangerment, they are legally required to report it to the appropriate state child protective services agency. This is not optional and applies regardless of the source of suspected abuse.

Mandated reporting is a legal obligation that overrides confidentiality. We will handle all reports with sensitivity and professionalism.
